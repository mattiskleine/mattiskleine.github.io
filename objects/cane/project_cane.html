Project Requirements
  • Theme: bodily support
  • Focus: user-centered design
  • Emphasis: ergonomics & mechanics

The scope of this 1st semster project led me to working closely together with a local Parkinson's club in order to address the problematic posture Parkinson's patients develop. Turns out their bodies are actually still capable of a good upright posture. It is the feedback about the body's current posture that goes missing, which then slowly but surely leads to the typical hunch.

Collaboration with various physiotherapists helped
me to design a cane, which senses a bad posture
and gives a gentle reminder to straighten up.

Posture Sensor

The sensory system is very simple and extremely reliable due to its chipless nature. An internal spring mechanism located at the bottom of the cane restrains a pin from touching two ends of a wire, unless a certain amount of force is applied. If the pin touches the two wire ends, it acts as a switch, closing a circuit that activates a tiny vibration motor inside the handle. The required force depends on the spring constant, which in turn needs to be adjusted for each individual user.

Self-standing

The initial user studies highlighted a problem many cane users have in common: where to leave it when sitting down. Leaning it against the chair results in frequent tip-overs whenever the chair is moved.

Self-standing canes are nothing new, but they are ugly! Me and my team came up with a stylish design utilizing a ball-and-socket joint and another spring mechanism to ensure stable use, good looks and a fairly firm self-standing feature.
